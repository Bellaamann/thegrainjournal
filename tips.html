What I Look For When Choosing a Film Stock

Lighting conditions: I think about where I’m shooting: bright sun, cloudy days, indoors, golden hour, etc. Different stocks handle light so differently.

ISO/speed: Lower ISO (100 to 200) for crisp detail in bright light; higher ISO (400 to 800+) for low light or more grainy, moody vibes.

Color vs. black & white: If I want rich colors or warm tones, I pick a color stock; if I want classic mood or texture, I switch to black and white.

Grain level: Fine grain for clean, sharp photos; heavier grain when I want that vintage, gritty character.

Skin tone rendering: Some films render skin super warm and soft, others punchier or more contrasty.

Contrast & saturation: I pick stocks based on whether I want soft or flat tones for editing flexibility or bold, high contrast pops straight from the scan.

Budget & availability: Film is pricey, so I choose stocks that fit the shoot without blowing my budget.

Fun Film Photography Facts

Every film stock has its own personality: Two shots in the same light with different films can look totally different.

Film latitude: How much detail a film keeps in shadows and highlights is a game changer. Some films forgive mistakes, some do not.

Most films look even better slightly overexposed: Especially color negative films.

Film grain isn’t a flaw: It’s part of the film’s physical texture, which is why it looks so organic compared to digital noise.

Expired film can give wild results: Color shifts, weird tones, extra grain, depending on how it was stored.

Medium format film = higher quality: The larger negative gives smoother colors and less grain.

Many film stocks are decades old: Some formulas haven’t changed much since the 1970s or earlier.
